Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMCs) isolation

The purpose of cell separation is to distinguish mononuclear cells from peripheral blood, which is to be able to study the individual cells more closely with a view to cell differentiation.

Blood and the cells it contains

If a test tube of blood is left to stand for half an hour, the blood separates into three layers as the denser components sink to the bottom of the tube and fluid remains at the top. The straw-colored fluid that forms the top layer is called plasma. The middle white layer is composed of white blood cells (WBCs) and platelets, and the bottom red layer is the red blood cells (RBCs). All of the cells found in the blood come from bone marrow. They begin their life as stem cells, and they mature into three main types of cells‚Äî RBCs, WBCs, and platelets. In turn, there are three types of WBC‚Äîlymphocytes, monocytes, and granulocytes‚Äîand three main types of granulocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils).

Methods for mononuclear isolation

The prerequisite for the fixed cellular immunological studies are purified populations of the various cell types involved in the immune response (innate or adaptive immunity). Whether the starting point is blood or lymphoid tissue, an initial purification step will often consist of isolating a cell population which is usually called mononuclear cells: (MC, MonoVuclear Cells). Other abbreviations are also used for these cells, Peripheral Blood Leukocytes (PBL) or Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMC). PBMCs include lymphocytes (i.e. T cells, B cells, and NK cells), monocytes, and dendritic cells, and are defined as white blood cells with round nuclei. One of the most widely used methods for PBMC isolation is density gradient centrifugation. Differences in cell density are the principle used to isolate mononuclear cells from other cells in the blood. Using this technique, scientists add blood to a density medium in a tube and centrifuge it. The density medium is a pre-prepared sterile buffer solution with a very specific density optimized for layer stratification. The centrifugal force created at high spinning speeds separates the blood components based on their density compared to the solution. RBCs have a slightly higher density, spinning to the bottom of the tube, while PBMCs have a slightly lower density, leaving them to float on top of the density medium, and plasma will be in the top layer. After centrifugation, the layer containing PBMCs is carefully collected with a pipette for further use, leaving the unwanted RBCs and plasma behind.

The definition of Lymphocytes

Lymphocytes are a type of leukocytes (white blood cells) and the main functional cells of the immune system. They are typically small and have large, round basophilic nuclei with a thin rim of pale staining, basophilic cytoplasm abundant, representing ~20-40% of the circulating leukocytes populations, and are relatively easy to find in a smear.

Types of Lymphocytes

Lymphocytes are subdivided into 3 functional groups based on their distinctive surface molecules called cluster of differentiation, or CD markers. These groups include B lymphocytes (B cells), T lymphocytes (T cells) and Natural killer (NK) cells. B lymphocytes (B cells), produce antibodies in response to foreign antigens (fragments or whole bacteria, viruses and parasites). T lymphocytes (T cells), are involved in cell-mediated immunity in response to intracellular pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites), tumor cells and, at times, surgical implants. Natural killer (NK) cells are involved in the innate immune system and are responsible for killing certain virus-infected cells and several types of tumor cells. B and T cells are the main types of lymphocytes, cannot be distinguished in routinely prepared blood smears. However, T-lymphocytes express CD3, CD4, CD8, or CD25 markers. B-lymphocytes express CD19 marker. The expression of different CD-markers allows the separation/ differentiation of T and B cells.
