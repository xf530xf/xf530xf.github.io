Treponema pallidum and Syphilis

Syphilis (lues) occurs only in humans. Treponema pallidum subsp. pallidum is the causative agent of syphilis. Infection occurs by direct contact with infected lesions via microtraumas of the skin or mucous membrane. The disease progresses through three stages. After 5 - 90 days (average 21 days), a lesion appears at the site of infection (primary stage, hard chancre, lues I). The secondary stage (lues II) develops 3 - 6 weeks later. The tertiary stage (lues III) is marked by manifestations of the skin, mucous membranes, and various organs and affects about one third of the untreated syphilis patients. There are long latent periods between the stages. Infected people are contagious during the first 2 stages. During treponemal infection, Immunoglobulin M (IgM) class antibodies are usually the first to be produced and Immunoglobulin G (IgG) class antibodies increases with the duration of this disease. Serologic assays for syphilis diagnosis fall into two categories: non-treponemal and treponemal. Treponemal tests are qualitative assays performed on serum to detect antibodies (usually IgG but occasionally IgM) against a variety of T. pallidum antigens; these antibodies are detectable 2 to 4‚Äâweeks after exposure. Nontreponemal tests are performed on serially diluted serum to detect total antibodies (IgM and IgG) directed against lipoidal antigens, such as cardiolipin and lecithin, which are released from damaged host cells and the bacteria.

Methods for diagnosing Syphilis

Results of treponemal tests are typically reported as reactive or nonreactive without any titers. Treponemal tests are, in general, more sensitive in early infection, and once positive, they usually remain reactive indefinitely. They cannot be used to monitor treatment response or diagnose reinfection due to poor correlation with disease activity. They also cannot distinguish syphilis from infections with endemic Treponema, such as yaws (T. pallidum subsp. pertenue) and pinta (T. carateum) due to treponemal species cross-reaction (Satyaputra et al., 2021). Examples of treponemal tests include the fluorescent antibody absorption (FTA-ABS), T. pallidum particle agglutination (TPPA), T. pallidum hemagglutination (TPHA), microhemagglutination assay for T. pallidum (MHA-TP) and enzyme immunoassay (EIA) methods. As opposed to treponemal tests, nontreponemal tests are quantitative and reported in titers. Examples of nontreponemal tests include the Venereal Disease Research Laboratory (VDRL), rapid plasma reagin (RPR), unheated serum reagin (USR), and toluidine red unheated serum test (TRUST) methods (Satyaputra et al., 2021). An initial workup for syphilis includes either a nontreponemal screening assay followed by a confirmatory treponemal test (traditional sequence), or an automated treponemal test followed by a confirmatory nontreponemal test (reverse sequence). In the reverse sequence, discordant results must be confirmed by another treponemal test.
